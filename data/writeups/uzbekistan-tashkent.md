## Overview
Uzbekistan Tashkent is a Central Asian intermediate landrace from the Tashkent Region of Uzbekistan, associated with the capital city and its surrounding fertile agricultural zone in the foothills of the western Tian Shan. Tashkent sits at the crossroads of the Fergana Valley and the broader Uzbek steppe, historically one of the most important Central Asian cities on the Silk Road and a major hub connecting Afghan, Kazakh, and Chinese cannabis populations.

## History
Cannabis cultivation in the Tashkent region reflects Uzbekistan's long position as a Central Asian trading and agricultural center. Grower accounts and ethnobotanical records suggest that cannabis populations in the Tashkent area were shaped by Silk Road trade routes bringing diverse genetic material from Afghanistan to the south, Kazakhstan to the north, and China to the east. The intermediate indica-sativa morphology of this population is consistent with a gene pool that has absorbed influences from multiple directions. Selection in the fertile, irrigated Tashkent River valley likely favored productive, adaptable plants suited to the continental growing season. Specific documentation of a distinct Tashkent cultivar is limited.

## Description
- Intermediate indica-sativa morphology; medium-tall stature suited to irrigated steppe agriculture.
- Adapted to fertile river valley soils and the hot summers of the Tashkent region.
- Likely exhibits good drought tolerance alongside irrigation-boosted vigor.
- Variable phenotypic expression consistent with an intermediate, crossroads population.

## Grow Information
The Tashkent Region experiences an irrigated continental steppe climate: hot, dry summers reaching 35–40°C, cold winters, and moderate precipitation supplemented by Chirchiq River irrigation. Cannabis here is adapted to warm-season cultivation with good soil fertility. Flowering is typically reported at 9–13 weeks. The intermediate character of this population makes it adaptable to a range of growing conditions between pure indica and pure sativa regimens. Its Silk Road heritage suggests broad genetic heterogeneity and potential for interesting phenotypic variation.
