## Overview

The Cape York Peninsula feral cannabis population is found in the northernmost extent of Queensland, Australia — a remote, sparsely populated region of tropical savanna, monsoon forest, and wetlands that extends toward the Torres Strait and Papua New Guinea. This isolated northern Australian feral population is notable for its adaptation to intense heat, seasonal drought, and the harsh conditions of Australia's tropical north. Its isolation from both cultivated cannabis populations and the more temperate southern Australian feral populations suggests potentially unique adaptive traits have developed over time.

## History

The origins of cannabis on Cape York Peninsula are not formally documented. The region's proximity to Papua New Guinea and the Torres Strait islands has led to speculation that cannabis may have arrived via maritime contact at some point in the past, though this remains unverified. The feral status of this population indicates it has persisted and naturalized without sustained human cultivation. Given the remoteness of Cape York Peninsula — with limited road access and vast tracts of conservation land — this population has likely experienced minimal genetic contamination from introduced cultivated varieties.

## Description

- **Type:** Feral sativa.
- **Height:** Tall, consistent with sativa-type growth in a tropical environment.
- **Flowering:** 10–14 weeks, adapted to the strong seasonal photoperiod shift of Australia's tropical north.
- **Climate:** Tropical savanna — pronounced wet and dry seasons, high temperatures, intense UV exposure, periodic drought stress.
- **Potential traits:** Grower accounts and regional context suggest heat and drought tolerance as likely adaptations; genetic variability within the feral population is expected to be considerable.

## Grow Information

Cape York Peninsula cannabis, as a tropical savanna-adapted feral population, would likely perform best in hot, seasonally dry conditions with full sun exposure. The 10–14 week flowering window aligns with the tropical savanna photoperiod cycle. Drought tolerance is inferred from the harsh dry season conditions of the region. Cultivators working with this material should anticipate high phenotypic variability given its feral classification and limited selection history. This population is of potential preservation interest given its geographic isolation and the possibility of locally unique adaptive traits.
