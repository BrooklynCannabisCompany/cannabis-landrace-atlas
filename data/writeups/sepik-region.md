## Overview

Sepik Region is a feral sativa population associated with the Sepik River area of northern Papua New Guinea. The Sepik is one of the longest rivers in the Pacific, draining a vast lowland basin characterised by equatorial heat, high rainfall, and dense tropical vegetation. As a feral population, this entry represents cannabis plants growing without ongoing human cultivation management in the Sepik's tropical river basin environment — distinct from formally preserved or cultivated landrace selections from the same broad region.

## History

Feral cannabis populations in the Sepik River region are thought to descend from plants originally introduced or cultivated by local communities, which subsequently naturalised in the surrounding lowland environment. The region's remoteness and the density of its river-basin ecosystem have allowed feral populations to persist and adapt with minimal outside genetic input. The timeline and pathway of original introduction to this part of Papua New Guinea are not well documented. Because this entry is categorised as feral rather than a maintained cultivar, specific selection history or collector provenance is not available.

## Description

Sepik Region plants are classified as a feral sativa with a very tall growth habit, consistent with sativa populations naturalised in an equatorial lowland environment where competition for light in dense vegetation favours vertical growth. A flowering period of 13–16 weeks is somewhat shorter than the related Sepik Basin PNG cultivar entry, potentially reflecting adaptation to the specific microenvironmental pressures of feral rather than cultivated conditions. Open bud structure suited to high ambient humidity is expected.

- **Height:** Very tall
- **Flowering:** 13–16 weeks
- **Climate preference:** Tropical river basin
- **Category:** Feral

## Grow Information

As a feral tropical sativa, Sepik Region plants are likely to perform best in environments approximating their equatorial lowland origin — sustained heat, high humidity, and stable long days. The somewhat shorter flowering window compared to some PNG populations (13–16 weeks) may offer a modest practical advantage for growers with long growing seasons. Indoor cultivation is possible but the very tall stature demands significant vertical space. Little cultivated-growth data is available for this feral population; growers should expect natural vigour and variability characteristic of unselected feral plants rather than the consistency of a stabilised cultivar.
