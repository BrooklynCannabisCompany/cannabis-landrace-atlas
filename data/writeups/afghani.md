## Overview
Afghani is a broad-leaf indica landrace from the mountains of Afghanistan, long
associated with traditional hashish production. It is one of the foundational
populations behind much of modern indica breeding, valued for dense, resin-heavy
flowers and a compact, sturdy structure.

## History
Grower accounts and breeding lore commonly credit Afghan hash-producing populations
as a primary source of the indica genetics that spread into Western breeding programs
from the 1970s onward. Selection in Afghanistan reportedly favored heavy resin (for
charas and hashish) and short stature suited to a cold, arid mountain climate. Precise
lineage is undocumented and varies by valley and seed line.

## Description
- **Morphotype:** Broad-Leaf Drug
- **Vernacular type:** Indica
- **Chemotype:** Type I (inferred)
- **Domestication:** Domesticated
- **Height:** Short
- **Flowering:** 7–9 weeks
- **Climate:** Desert / Arid
- **Origin:** Afghanistan (Middle East / Central Asia)

Broad-leaf morphology; short and bushy with thick stems. Dense, resinous flowers; aromas commonly described as earthy, hashy, and sweet. Effects are typically reported as heavy and relaxing.

## Grow Information
Adapted to a cold, arid mountain environment. Commonly reported as short with a fast
flowering window (roughly 7–9 weeks) and abundant resin. Its compact size and quick
finish are traits growers associate with Afghan indica stock; humidity tolerance is
generally lower than that of tropical sativas, so dense flowers can be mold-prone in
wet climates.
