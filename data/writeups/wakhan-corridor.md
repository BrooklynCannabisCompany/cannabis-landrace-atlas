## Overview
Wakhan Corridor is a highland landrace complex from the Wakhan Corridor of northeastern Afghanistan, the long narrow panhandle of territory that extends eastward between Tajikistan and Pakistan to meet China's Xinjiang region. This remote, high-altitude valley — largely populated by Wakhi and Kyrgyz communities — sits between the Hindu Kush and Pamir mountain systems at elevations of 2,800–4,500 meters, making it one of the most geographically isolated cannabis-growing zones in the world.

## History
The Wakhan Corridor's extreme isolation — accessible historically only by difficult mountain passes — has likely contributed to the genetic distinctiveness of any cannabis populations found there. Situated between Afghanistan's Hindu Kush hash-producing populations and the Pamir populations of Tajikistan, the Wakhan's gene pool is commonly thought to reflect ancient mountain landrace genetics with limited outside influence in recent centuries. Grower accounts and preservation-focused collectors note this region as an important candidate for genetic conservation, given that its isolation may have preserved diversity lost elsewhere. Specific documented cultivation history for this exact corridor is very sparse.

## Description
- Highland landrace complex; medium stature adapted to extreme altitude.
- Mixed morphology reflecting the corridor position between Afghan Hindu Kush and Pamir genetic zones.
- Strong cold tolerance is the primary adaptive trait; plants must survive frosts and complete maturation in a short alpine growing window.
- Dense, potentially resinous flowers consistent with traditional Afghan-adjacent hash-producing populations.

## Grow Information
The Wakhan Corridor's alpine mountain valley climate is extreme: very cold winters, a short frost-free season typically from late spring to early autumn, intense UV radiation at altitude, and thin mountain soils. Cannabis populations here must complete their lifecycle rapidly under these constraints. Flowering is typically reported at 10–14 weeks. The cold tolerance and geographic isolation of this population make it a high-priority candidate for preservation collections. Growers interested in extreme cold-climate genetics will find the Wakhan Corridor one of the most compelling populations in the Hindu Kush-Pamir zone.
