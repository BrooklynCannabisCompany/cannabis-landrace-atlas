## Overview

The Mexican Sierra Madre Complex refers to the broad genetic corridor of sativa landraces distributed across the Sierra Madre mountain ranges of Mexico — encompassing the Sierra Madre Occidental in the west, the Sierra Madre Oriental in the east, and the Sierra Madre del Sur in the south. This is a complex designation rather than a single discrete population, reflecting the reality that cannabis genetics across these vast mountain ranges share common traits while also varying considerably by locality and altitude. The corridor runs through the states most historically associated with Mexican cannabis cultivation, creating a distributed gene pool shaped by diverse mountain microclimates.

## History

Mexico has one of the longest documented cannabis cultivation histories in the Western Hemisphere, with introduction likely occurring in the colonial era via Spanish trade routes. The Sierra Madre ranges served as both geographic barriers and cultivation corridors, with isolated mountain communities developing locally adapted populations over generations. The 20th century saw intensive cultivation across these ranges, particularly in states like Sinaloa, Durango, Guerrero, Oaxaca, and Chihuahua, all of which intersect with the Sierra Madre systems. These genetics contributed significantly to the North American cannabis market and to early breeding programs, forming part of the foundation for many modern varieties.

## Description
- **Origin:** Mexico (Americas)
- **Climate:** Mountain range; diverse microclimates across a broad elevation gradient
- **Morphotype:** Narrow-Leaf Drug
- **Chemotype:** Type I (inferred)
- **Domestication:** Domesticated
- **Vernacular type:** Sativa
- **Height:** Very tall
- **Flowering Time:** 12–16 weeks

As a complex, this designation encompasses significant phenotypic variability, but common traits reported across Sierra Madre populations include very tall stature, long internodal spacing, and extended flowering cycles suited to the mountain photoperiod. The 12–16 week flowering window reflects the latitude and elevation of these mountain ranges.

## Grow Information

The broad genetic diversity within the Sierra Madre Complex means that growth characteristics can vary considerably between populations. In general, plants from this corridor tend to be very tall, late-flowering sativas requiring significant space and a long season. Outdoor cultivation in warm, dry-summer mountain climates is most aligned with their origin. Many regional variants within this complex may have specific adaptations to local conditions — altitude, rainfall, and temperature regimes vary substantially across the Sierra Madre ranges.
