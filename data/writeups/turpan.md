## Overview
Turpan is a mixed oasis landrace from the Turpan Basin (also spelled Turfan) in China's Xinjiang region — one of the lowest, hottest, and most arid places on Earth, lying below sea level in a depression rimmed by mountains. The oasis city of Turpan has been an important Silk Road stop for millennia, and cannabis cultivated in its extreme environment represents one of the most heat- and drought-adapted populations documented.

## History
Turpan's position as a key oasis on the Silk Road means it has been a transit point for goods, peoples, and plant material — including cannabis — for over two thousand years. Cannabis cultivation in the Turpan oasis is likely ancient, sustained by the region's elaborate traditional irrigation systems (the karez underground channels) that bring glacial meltwater from the surrounding mountains. The extreme climate would have exerted strong selection pressure toward drought-adapted, heat-tolerant phenotypes. The mixed classification suggests the population reflects influences from multiple directions along the trade route. Specific documentation of the Turpan cannabis population is limited.

## Description
- **Origin:** China (Middle East / Central Asia)
- **Climate:** Desert / Arid (recorded as “Desert oasis”)
- **Morphotype:** Unclassified
- **Chemotype:** Type I (inferred)
- **Domestication:** Domesticated
- **Vernacular type:** Mixed
- **Height:** Medium-tall
- **Flowering Time:** 9–13 weeks

Mixed oasis landrace; medium-tall stature adapted to irrigated desert conditions. Exceptional drought tolerance and heat resilience; among the most extreme climate adaptations documented. Variable morphology reflecting the crossroads nature of Silk Road genetic influence. Likely well-suited to alkaline, mineral-rich desert soils typical of oasis agriculture.

## Grow Information
The Turpan Basin climate is extreme: summer temperatures regularly exceed 40°C, annual rainfall is minimal (around 16 mm), and the basin sits below sea level with intense reflected heat. Cannabis thriving here is sustained entirely by irrigation and is adapted to one of the planet's harshest agricultural environments. Flowering is typically reported at 9–13 weeks. The combination of intense heat, minimal humidity, and strong sunlight shapes a population of exceptional interest for drought-climate breeding. Low humidity and high-heat conditions should be replicated for best results when growing this population outside its native range.
