## Overview
Trans-Baikal Wild Cannabis is a ruderalis population from the Transbaikal (Zabaykalsky) region of Russia, a vast territory of steppe, taiga, and semi-arid grasslands east of Lake Baikal, bordering Mongolia and China. This is among the northernmost and easternmost cannabis populations documented in Central Asia, persisting as wild plants in one of the most climatically extreme inhabited regions on Earth.

## History
Wild ruderalis cannabis has long been documented across the Russian steppe and Siberian transition zones, where short summers and extreme cold have driven adaptation toward compact size, early maturation, and automatic flowering. The Transbaikal region's cannabis is generally understood to be ancient naturalized populations, potentially present since the Bronze Age via Eurasian steppe peoples who moved cannabis eastward. No intentional cultivation or selection history is documented for this specific population; it represents survival under extreme natural conditions.

## Description
- **Origin:** Russia (Middle East / Central Asia)
- **Climate:** Steppe / Semi-arid (recorded as “Siberian steppe”)
- **Morphotype:** Ruderalis (wild-type)
- **Chemotype:** Type II (inferred)
- **Domestication:** Wild
- **Vernacular type:** Ruderalis
- **Height:** Short
- **Flowering Time:** 6-10 weeks

Short stature; classic ruderalis morphology with small leaves and minimal branching. Automatic flowering — day-length independent — enabling completion during the brief Siberian summer. Extreme cold tolerance; adapted to frost-prone conditions with a very short warm season. Likely very low psychoactive potency, as is typical of wild ruderalis populations selected primarily for survival rather than resin.

## Grow Information
The Siberian steppe climate of the Transbaikal is defined by extremely cold winters (among the coldest inhabited regions on Earth), short warm summers, and significant drought stress. Cannabis surviving here must germinate, grow, flower, and set seed within a narrow window. Flowering is reported at 6–10 weeks. Short stature and automatic flowering are essential adaptive traits. This population's value to breeders lies primarily in its extreme cold tolerance and ruderalis genetics, useful for developing cold-hardy auto-flowering cultivars.
