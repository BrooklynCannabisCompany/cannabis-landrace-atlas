## Overview

The Ural Foothills population is a feral Ruderalis-type cannabis found in the foothills of the Ural Mountains in Russia, the natural boundary between Europe and Asia. This is a northern, cold-adapted population growing at latitudes where summer seasons are short and winters are severe. Plants of this type are typically short-statured and have been shaped over many generations by the harsh continental temperate climate of the region rather than by deliberate cultivation.

## History

Cannabis has grown wild across the Ural region for centuries, likely originating from a combination of escaped hemp cultivation and naturally spreading feral populations. The Ural foothills sit at an ecological boundary zone where cold, short growing seasons would have strongly selected for early-finishing, auto-flowering characteristics. Grower accounts suggest that Ruderalis populations from this latitude have attracted interest mainly as a source of autoflowering genetics rather than as a cultivated crop in their own right.

## Description

- **Type:** Ruderalis
- **Height:** Short — typically compact, low-growing plants adapted to exposed foothill terrain
- **Stature:** Sturdy, with relatively large leaf-to-stem ratios commonly reported in Ruderalis ecotypes
- **Flowering:** 6–10 weeks; likely initiates flowering based on age rather than photoperiod, consistent with Ruderalis adaptation to the short Ural summers
- **Potency:** Generally considered low in THC, as is typical for Ruderalis populations; cannabinoid profiles are not well documented for this specific population

## Grow Information

- **Climate:** Continental temperate — plants from this population are commonly reported to tolerate frost, cold nights, and the short growing windows characteristic of the Ural foothills
- **Autoflowering:** The short, age-dependent flowering cycle means these plants can complete their life cycle within a single brief summer season without requiring a shift in light hours
- **Outdoor suitability:** Well suited to high-latitude outdoor cultivation where long-season varieties would fail to finish
- **Indoor:** The compact size and rapid cycle make it manageable indoors, though yields are modest
- **Resistance:** Grower accounts suggest strong resistance to cold and variable weather, consistent with the population's origins
