## Overview
Tuva Feral is a cold-adapted feral hemp population from the Republic of Tuva (Tyva), a federal subject of Russia located in south-central Siberia, bordering Mongolia. Tuva sits in a landlocked basin surrounded by mountain ranges, producing an extreme continental climate that is one of the coldest inhabited regions in Asia outside of northeastern Siberia. Cannabis naturalized in this environment represents an exceptionally cold-hardy gene pool.

## History
Cannabis is understood to have naturalized across the southern Siberian steppe and forest-steppe zones over centuries, associated with both ancient trade connections and historical agricultural use by nomadic and semi-sedentary peoples of the region. Tuva's geographic position along ancient Eurasian steppe routes connecting the Altai region, Mongolia, and the broader Central Asian interior suggests cannabis arrived via steppe migrations in pre-modern times. The feral hemp populations of Tuva have adapted without cultivation management to the harsh local climate. Early maturation tendencies noted in the population likely reflect selection pressure for completion of the lifecycle before Tuvan winters arrive. Specific documentation is very limited.

## Description
- Feral hemp morphology; medium-tall stature with characteristic cold-adapted structure.
- Cold tolerance is the primary defining trait; adapted to Siberian steppe conditions.
- Early maturation tendencies suggest possible ruderalis influence or strong natural selection for rapid flowering.
- Likely low psychoactive potency, consistent with feral hemp populations selected primarily for survival.

## Grow Information
Tuva experiences an extreme continental climate: very cold winters (sometimes reaching -50°C in the basin), short summers, and significant temperature swings. Precipitation is low, and growing seasons are brief. This feral population must complete its lifecycle within the narrow warm window available. Flowering is reported at 10–14 weeks, though early-maturing phenotypes within the population may finish considerably faster. The potential ruderalis influence makes this population of interest for cold-climate and auto-flowering breeding programs. Hardiness and resilience under minimal care conditions are expected strengths.
